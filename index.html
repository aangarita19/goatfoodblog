Poblanos with pepitas

April 18, 2014

Poblanos are an underserved pepper. They have the satisfying crunch of a bell pepper and actually pack a bit of heat. They share that beautiful deep green color with the jalapeños and serranos, but don’t punish you with such an intense heat.

That means they are a perfect pepper for this simple preparation, which is actually a riff on a shisito pepper dish I learned during my time at The Tripel.

I started by cutting the poblanos into long strips and slicing half of a red onion into a nice julienne. Get a pan raging hot (I used my Lodge cast iron skillet) with a 1/2 tsp. of a neutral oil. When you see a whisp of smoke, add the onions and poblanos and let them sit undisturbed long enough to make you feel uncomfortable without really burning them (about 2 minutes).

Next add about a tablespoon of butter cut into small chunks, and start moving the poblanos and onions as the butter melts and lightly coats the veg. Keep moving for 4-5 more minutes until the red onions are well caramelized and the poblanos are cooked but still crunchy. Squeeze the juice of 1 lime to deglaze, and season with salt to taste.

Top with the pepitas below and enjoy.

...

The pipitas add a different kind of crunch to the dish. Garlic salt is a crucial part of my family’s pantry, and thus my own. The stuff is probably sacrilegious in serious culinary circles, since I guess you can get similar flavors by dehydrating your own garlic and parsley, processing it, making it into salt…(actually, this might have just become a new project of mine).

Nonetheless, I love garlic salt and it provides the perfect crust for these crunchy little pipitas.

Place a small pan over medium high heat and add your pipitas, 1 tsp. of oil, 1/2 tsp. of garlic salt, and 1/2 tsp. of ground ancho chili powder (any chili powder with medium heat will do well here). The pipitas will start to sizzle and pop. Sauté until the pepitas are well coated and golden brown. Drain on a paper towel to remove excess oil and reserve.
